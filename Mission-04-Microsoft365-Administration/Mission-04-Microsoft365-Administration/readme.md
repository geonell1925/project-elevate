# Reflection

Mission 4 significantly changed the way I approach Microsoft 365 administration. Before this mission, I often thought about troubleshooting by immediately checking settings or making configuration changes. Throughout the scenarios, I learned the importance of slowing down, understanding the scope of the incident, and allowing evidence to guide the investigation before taking action.

One of the biggest improvements in my thinking was recognizing the difference between authentication and authorization. If a user can successfully sign in to Microsoft 365, authentication through Microsoft Entra ID has already been completed. My investigation should then shift toward licensing, provisioning, permissions, group membership, or the specific Microsoft 365 workload rather than repeatedly focusing on sign-in issues.

Another lesson that became consistent throughout every scenario was identifying the scope of the problem. Determining whether an issue affects a single user, multiple users, or the entire organization immediately narrows the investigation and prevents unnecessary troubleshooting. This simple habit helped eliminate many possible causes before reviewing technical configurations.

Mission 4 also reinforced the importance of understanding user lifecycle management. Administrative actions such as onboarding, department transfers, licensing changes, group membership updates, and permission assignments often introduce unexpected issues. Asking "What changed before the problem started?" became one of the most valuable troubleshooting questions throughout this mission.

Comparing an affected user with a known working user also became one of my preferred troubleshooting techniques. Rather than guessing, I learned to compare licensing, group membership, Exchange Online permissions, Microsoft Teams membership, SharePoint permissions, and provisioning status to quickly identify differences that explain the issue.

Perhaps the most valuable lesson from this mission was learning to troubleshoot like a Microsoft 365 Administrator instead of simply resolving individual user problems. Every scenario followed the same engineering process: determine the business impact, identify the scope, eliminate healthy services, gather evidence, identify the root cause, validate the resolution, and document the findings. This structured methodology gives me greater confidence when approaching real production incidents and technical interviews.

Mission 4 strengthened my understanding of Microsoft 365 administration while reinforcing disciplined troubleshooting, evidence-based decision making, and the importance of following a consistent engineering methodology rather than relying on assumptions.
